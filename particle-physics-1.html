60-Day Guided Course

Particle Physics – 60-Day Roadmap

A step-by-step journey through modern particle physics: from relativity and quantum mechanics foundations to the Standard Model, QCD, detectors, and hints of physics beyond the Standard Model. Designed for consistent daily progress with clear tasks each day.

1. Course Overview – Major Modules

The course is split into nine modules over 60 days. Each day you cover one focused topic and complete a small, concrete task.

  • Module 1 (Days 1–7): Foundations of Modern Physics – classical vs quantum, relativity basics, natural units, and what “particles” actually mean.
  • Module 2 (Days 8–14): Quantum Mechanics Essentials – wave–particle duality, uncertainty, states & operators, spin.
  • Module 3 (Days 15–21): Relativity & 4-Vectors – energy–momentum, invariants, scattering kinematics.
  • Module 4 (Days 22–28): The Standard Model Zoo – quarks, leptons, gauge bosons, Higgs, forces as gauge interactions.
  • Module 5 (Days 29–35): Symmetries & Quantum Numbers – Noether’s theorem idea, charges, baryon/lepton number, C, P, T.
  • Module 6 (Days 36–42): Interactions & Feynman Diagrams – vertices, propagators, cross sections, decay widths.
  • Module 7 (Days 43–49): Hadrons, QCD & Confinement – mesons, baryons, color, strong interaction, partons.
  • Module 8 (Days 50–56): Detectors & Experiments – collider types, detector subsystems, event reconstruction, landmark discoveries.
  • Module 9 (Days 57–60): Beyond SM & Review – neutrino masses, dark matter, open questions, global wrap-up.

Module 1

Foundations of Modern Physics (Days 1–7)

Goal: Understand why we need relativity and quantum mechanics and how they reshape the notion of “particle”.

Day 1 – What is Particle Physics?Key ideas

Particle physics studies the fundamental constituents of matter and the forces between them:

  • Different from atomic, nuclear, or condensed-matter physics (which deal with bound or many-body systems).
  • Historical arcs: classical → relativity & early quantum → modern collider era.

Do this today

  • Write a half-page answer to: “What questions is particle physics trying to answer?”
  • List all elementary particles you’ve heard of (even if incomplete).
Day 2 – Particles vs Fields

Classical particle: point with position \( x(t) \) and momentum \( p(t) \). Field: quantity defined at every point in space and time (e.g. electric field).

Modern view: a “particle” is a quantum excitation of an underlying field.

Do this today

Draw a line from “pure point particles” to “pure fields”. Place:

  • billiard balls
  • electric field
  • photon
  • electron

Explain where you placed each and why.

Day 3 – Why Special Relativity?

Problems with Newtonian mechanics + Maxwell’s equations:

  • Maxwell predicts a constant speed of light \( c \) in vacuum.
  • Galilean relativity cannot keep \( c \) invariant for all observers.

Einstein’s postulates:

  • Laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames.
  • Speed of light in vacuum is the same for all inertial observers.

Leads to time dilation, length contraction, and relativity of simultaneity.

Do this today

Describe a “light clock” (two mirrors, light bouncing) and explain qualitatively why time must dilate when seen from another frame.

Day 4 – Relativistic Energy & Momentum

Energy–momentum relation:

$$E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$$

  • Rest energy: \(E_0 = mc^2\).
  • Massless particles (like photons): \(E = pc\).

Do this today

Electron mass \(\approx 0.511\ \text{MeV}/c^2\). Its rest energy is:

$$E_0 = mc^2 \approx 0.511\ \text{MeV}$$

Write in words what “rest energy” means physically for an electron.

Day 5 – Birth of Quanta: Planck & Einstein

Planck introduced energy quantization to solve blackbody radiation: $$E = h\nu$$

Einstein proposed light is made of photons with energy \(E = h\nu\), explaining the photoelectric effect.

Do this today

In your own words, explain why increasing the intensity of low-frequency light does not eject electrons in the photoelectric effect, but increasing the frequency does.

Day 6 – Wave–Particle Duality

de Broglie hypothesis:

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$$

  • Particles (like electrons) have wave-like behavior (diffraction, interference).
  • For macroscopic objects (like a tennis ball), \(\lambda\) is tiny; wave effects are unobservable.

Do this today

Compare, qualitatively, the de Broglie wavelength of a fast electron vs a tennis ball. Which is larger and why?

Day 7 – Natural Units & Scale Sense

In particle physics, we often set \(c = \hbar = 1\). Then energy, mass, and momentum all share the same unit.

  • \(1\ \text{GeV} = 10^9\ \text{eV}\).
  • Proton mass \(\approx 0.94\ \text{GeV}\).
  • Electron mass \(\approx 0.511\ \text{MeV}\).

Do this today

Make a small “scale card” listing:

  • Electron mass, proton mass.
  • Typical nuclear energy scale (MeV).
  • Collider scale (e.g., LHC energies in TeV).

Keep this card for later weeks.

Module 2

Quantum Mechanics Essentials (Days 8–14)

Goal: Build conceptual tools of quantum mechanics needed in particle physics: states, wavefunctions, operators, uncertainty, spin, and statistics.

Day 8 – States & Wavefunctions

Quantum state: complete description of a system, represented as a vector \(|\psi\rangle\) in Hilbert space.

In position representation, the wavefunction \(\psi(x)\) gives a probability density:

$$P(x) = |\psi(x)|^2$$

Do this today

  • Write a paragraph comparing classical state (position & momentum) vs quantum state.
  • Sketch a 1D wavefunction showing regions of high and low probability.
Day 9 – Operators & Observables

Observables (position, momentum, energy) are represented by operators (e.g. \(\hat{x}, \hat{p}\)).

  • Eigenvalues of an operator = possible measurement outcomes.
  • Expectation value gives average outcome over many trials.

Do this today

  • Take a simple 2×2 diagonal matrix as an operator and find its eigenvalues.
  • Explain how eigenvalues correspond to measurement results in quantum mechanics.
Day 10 – Uncertainty Principle

Heisenberg uncertainty principle:

$$\Delta x\,\Delta p \gtrsim \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

It is not about bad instruments; it is a fundamental property of quantum systems.

Do this today

Describe why the uncertainty principle makes the idea of an exact, classical trajectory problematic for particles like electrons.

Day 11 – Superposition & Measurement

States can be superpositions:

$$|\psi\rangle = a|0\rangle + b|1\rangle,\quad |a|^2 + |b|^2 = 1$$

Measurement “collapses” the state to one of the basis states, with probabilities given by \(|a|^2\) and \(|b|^2\).

Do this today

  • Pick a concrete example, e.g. \(a = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}, b = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\).
  • Compute the probabilities of measuring 0 or 1.
Day 12 – Spin-½ Systems

Spin is intrinsic angular momentum (not literal spinning). Spin-½ particles (e.g. electrons) have two basic states: “up” and “down”.

Stern–Gerlach experiment splits a beam into discrete spin components.

Do this today

Draw a Stern–Gerlach setup and label:

  • Input beam.
  • Magnet.
  • Two output beams (spin up & spin down).

Explain briefly what it tells us about spin.

Day 13 – Identical Particles & Statistics

Identical quantum particles are indistinguishable:

  • Bosons: integer spin, symmetric wavefunctions, can occupy the same state.
  • Fermions: half-integer spin, antisymmetric wavefunctions, obey Pauli exclusion principle.

Do this today

Create a table with columns: particle, spin, boson/fermion (e.g. photon, electron, proton, Higgs, W, gluon).

Day 14 – Binding & Simple Potentials

Quantum particles in a potential well have:

  • Bound states with discrete energy levels.
  • Scattering states with continuous energy spectrum.

Do this today

Sketch a simple 1D potential well with a few quantized energy levels and label them.

Module 3

Relativity & Kinematics (Days 15–21)

Goal: Use 4-vectors and invariants to describe relativistic collisions and decays, and connect this to experimental observables.

Day 15 – 4-Vectors & Spacetime

Event in spacetime: \((ct, x, y, z)\). 4-vectors transform cleanly between inertial frames.

The invariant interval (Minkowski metric) remains the same in all inertial frames.

Do this today

Explain why combining space and time into 4-vectors simplifies relativistic physics compared to treating them separately.

Day 16 – Energy–Momentum 4-Vector

4-momentum:

$$p^\mu = \left(\frac{E}{c}, \vec{p}\right)$$

Invariant:

$$p^\mu p_\mu = m^2 c^2$$

Do this today

For an electron at rest, write its 4-momentum and note that \(p = 0\) while \(E = mc^2\).

Day 17 – Lab vs Center-of-Mass Frame

Lab frame: target at rest, projectile incoming. Center-of-mass (CM) frame: total momentum is zero; simplifies analysis.

Do this today

Explain why colliders (beam–beam) are more energy-efficient for creating heavy particles than fixed-target experiments.

Day 18 – Threshold Energy

Threshold: minimum CM energy needed to produce a certain final state:

$$E_{\text{CM}} \ge \sum m_{\text{final}} c^2$$

Do this today

If you want to create a particle of mass \(1\ \text{GeV}\) in a collider, what is at least the CM energy required? (Answer: \(\ge 1\ \text{GeV}\); in practice, more is needed.)

Day 19 – Relativistic Collisions & Invariant Mass

Invariant mass of a system:

$$M^2 c^4 = (E_{\text{tot}})^2 - (p_{\text{tot}} c)^2$$

Used to reconstruct particles from their decay products.

Do this today

Conceptually: if two photons are produced back-to-back with equal energies, what determines the invariant mass of the pair? Explain in words.

Day 20 – Time Dilation & Decay

Lifetime in rest frame: \(\tau_0\). In lab frame:

$$\tau = \gamma \tau_0$$

Moving particles can travel macroscopic distances before decaying.

Do this today

A particle with \(\tau_0 = 1\ \text{ns}\) has \(\gamma = 10\). Its lab lifetime is \(\tau = 10\ \text{ns}\). If its speed \(\approx c\), estimate how far it travels (about 3 m).

Day 21 – Review 1 (Weeks 1–3)

You should now grasp:

  • Why relativity and quantum mechanics are needed.
  • States, uncertainty, spin, and 4-vectors.
  • Energy–momentum relations and invariants.

Do this today

  • Write a one-page summary of the most important ideas so far.
  • Create a 10-question conceptual self-quiz and answer it.
Module 4

The Standard Model Particle Zoo (Days 22–28)

Goal: Learn the structure of the Standard Model: fermions, gauge bosons, and the Higgs sector.

Day 22 – Standard Model Overview

The Standard Model is a gauge theory based on $$SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$$

Main components:

  • Gauge fields (forces).
  • Matter fields (fermions).
  • Higgs field (gives masses).

Do this today

Sketch three labeled boxes: “Gluons (strong)”, “W/Z/γ (electroweak)”, “Higgs field”, and draw arrows to “quarks” and “leptons”.

Day 23 – Leptons

Three charged leptons: \(e, \mu, \tau\). Three neutrinos: \(\nu_e, \nu_\mu, \nu_\tau\).

Approximate conservation of lepton family numbers (electron, muon, tau).

Do this today

Make a table: particle, electric charge, approximate mass, lepton family (e, μ, τ).

Day 24 – Quarks & Color

Six quark flavors:

$$u, d, c, s, t, b$$

Charges:

  • Up-type: \(u, c, t\) with \(+2/3\,e\).
  • Down-type: \(d, s, b\) with \(-1/3\,e\).

Quarks carry color charge (red, green, blue) and are confined inside hadrons.

Do this today

Create a table listing each quark with electric charge, typical role (e.g., u/d in nucleons, s in strange hadrons, etc.).

Day 25 – Gauge Bosons

Force carriers:

  • Photon (\(\gamma\)) for electromagnetism.
  • W\(^{\pm}\), Z\(^{0}\) for weak interaction.
  • Eight gluons for strong interaction.

Do this today

Draw an “exchange” diagram for EM: two electrons exchanging a photon, labeling initial and final momenta qualitatively.

Day 26 – Higgs Field & Mass

The Higgs field has a nonzero vacuum expectation value; particles coupling to it acquire mass. Higgs boson = quantum excitation of the Higgs field.

Do this today

Write a non-technical explanation of how the Higgs gives mass, suitable for a smart high-school student, using analogies instead of equations.

Day 27 – Generations & Mixing

Fermions appear in three generations with increasing mass:

  • Leptons: \((\nu_e, e), (\nu_\mu, \mu), (\nu_\tau, \tau)\).
  • Quarks: \((u, d), (c, s), (t, b)\).

There is mixing among quarks (CKM) and neutrinos (PMNS), leading to phenomena like neutrino oscillations.

Do this today

Write the three generations explicitly and mark which are lightest vs heaviest.

Day 28 – Standard Model Classification Practice

Pull everything together: classify particles by spin, charge, mass scale, and role (matter, force, or Higgs).

Do this today

Make a “particle zoo poster” with sections for leptons, quarks, and bosons, listing their key properties.

Module 5

Symmetries & Quantum Numbers (Days 29–35)

Goal: See how symmetries lead to conservation laws and how quantum numbers organize allowed interactions.

Day 29 – Symmetries & Conservation Laws

Noether’s theorem (conceptual): continuous symmetries correspond to conserved quantities.

  • Time translations ↔ energy.
  • Space translations ↔ momentum.
  • Rotations ↔ angular momentum.

Do this today

Write three symmetry–conservation pairs with brief explanations.

Day 30 – Electric Charge, Baryon & Lepton Numbers

Many processes in the Standard Model conserve:

  • Electric charge \(Q\).
  • Baryon number \(B\).
  • Lepton number \(L\) (and approximately each family lepton number).

Do this today

For beta decay \(n \to p + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e\), verify conservation of:

  • Electric charge.
  • Baryon number.
  • Lepton number.
Day 31 – Isospin & Flavor Symmetries

Isospin: treat proton and neutron as a doublet under an approximate SU(2) symmetry.

SU(3) flavor symmetry for \(u, d, s\) was used in early quark model to classify hadrons.

Do this today

Explain why isospin is only approximate (hint: \(u\) and \(d\) have different masses and charges).

Day 32 – Discrete Symmetries: C, P, T

Discrete transformations:

  • P (parity): spatial inversion \( \vec{x} \to -\vec{x} \).
  • C (charge conjugation): particle ↔ antiparticle.
  • T (time reversal): \(t \to -t\).

Do this today

  • Give a real-life analogy of parity (a mirror reflection).
  • Write a short note on what parity violation in a process means physically.
Day 33 – CP Violation

Combined CP symmetry can be violated in weak interactions (e.g., in kaon and B-meson decays).

CP violation is a key ingredient in explaining why the universe is dominated by matter rather than antimatter (though the Standard Model’s CP violation is likely insufficient by itself).

Do this today

Write a paragraph explaining why CP violation is important for the matter–antimatter asymmetry in the universe.

Day 34 – Global vs Gauge Symmetries

Global symmetry: same transformation everywhere in spacetime. Gauge symmetry: transformation can vary from point to point; implies existence of gauge fields.

Do this today

Compare global vs gauge symmetries in three bullet points.

Day 35 – Selection Rules & Allowed/Forbidden Processes

Conservation laws impose “selection rules” on which processes can occur.

If a proposed reaction violates charge, baryon, or lepton number, it is forbidden in the Standard Model.

Do this today

Invent a hypothetical reaction and check if it conserves charge, \(B\), and \(L\). If not, explain which conservation law is violated.

Module 6

Interactions & Feynman Diagrams (Days 36–42)

Goal: Understand how interactions are represented by Feynman diagrams and related to cross sections and decay rates.

Day 36 – Fundamental Interactions Overview

Fundamental interactions (ignoring gravity here):

  • Strong: mediated by gluons.
  • Electromagnetic: mediated by photons.
  • Weak: mediated by W and Z bosons.

They differ in strength, range, and which particles they couple to.

Do this today

Make a table: interaction, mediator, relative strength, range.

Day 37 – What is a Feynman Diagram?

Feynman diagrams are pictorial representations of terms in a perturbative expansion of the amplitude.

  • External lines: incoming and outgoing real particles.
  • Internal lines: virtual particles.
  • Vertices: interaction points from the Lagrangian.

Do this today

Draw e\(^{-}\)e\(^{+}\) → μ\(^{-}\)μ\(^{+}\) via a single intermediate photon and label all external and internal lines.

Day 38 – Basic QED Diagrams

QED interaction vertex couples electrons/positrons to photons.

  • Examples: electron scattering, Compton scattering, pair production.
  • Virtual photons exchanged internally carry momentum but are off-shell.

Do this today

Draw 2–3 simple QED diagrams (e.g., electron–electron scattering, Compton scattering) and annotate the initial and final states.

Day 39 – Weak Interaction & Beta Decay

Charged current weak interactions involve W\(^{\pm}\), changing quark flavor.

Example: beta decay at quark level:

$$d \to u + W^- \quad,\quad W^- \to e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

Do this today

Draw the Feynman diagram for beta decay, labeling the d and u quarks, W boson, electron, and antineutrino.

Day 40 – Decays: Lifetime & Width

An unstable particle decays exponentially with lifetime \(\tau\).

Decay width \(\Gamma\) relates to lifetime:

$$\Gamma \approx \frac{\hbar}{\tau}$$

Branching ratios describe the fraction of decays into different channels.

Do this today

If a particle has \(\tau = 10^{-12}\ \text{s}\), estimate its width \(\Gamma\) symbolically using \(\Gamma \approx \hbar/\tau\) and discuss qualitatively what a larger \(\Gamma\) means.

Day 41 – Cross Sections

Cross section \(\sigma\) is an effective area measuring interaction probability.

Units: barns (\(1\ \text{b} = 10^{-28}\ \text{m}^2\)).

Event rate \(\sim\) (flux) × \(\sigma\) × (target density).

Do this today

Write a short explanation: why does a larger cross section, for the same beam and target, mean you detect more events?

Day 42 – From Diagrams to Observables

Steps (qualitative):

  • Write down Feynman diagrams for a process.
  • Compute amplitude(s) \(\mathcal{M}\).
  • Probability \(\propto |\mathcal{M}|^2\).
  • Integrate over phase space → cross section or decay rate.

Do this today

For e\(^{-}\)e\(^{+}\) → μ\(^{-}\)μ\(^{+}\), describe in words the chain: theory → diagram → amplitude → predicted cross section → comparison with experiment.

Module 7

QCD, Hadrons & Confinement (Days 43–49)

Goal: Recognize hadrons as composite states of quarks and gluons, and understand confinement and jets.

Day 43 – Hadrons vs Elementary Particles

Hadrons: composite particles made of quarks and gluons.

  • Mesons: quark–antiquark states.
  • Baryons: three-quark states.

Elementary particles: quarks, leptons, gauge bosons, Higgs.

Do this today

List at least 3 mesons and 3 baryons (e.g., p, n, π, K, etc.) and note their quark content (at least for p, n, π).

Day 44 – Quark Model & Classification

SU(3) flavor symmetries organize hadrons into multiplets (octets, decuplets, etc.) for \(u, d, s\).

Quark model explains patterns in masses and allowed decays.

Do this today

Sketch a simple baryon octet (triangle) and place p, n, and other light baryons in the diagram.

Day 45 – Color Confinement & Asymptotic Freedom

Color confinement: quarks and gluons are not observed as free particles; they are confined within hadrons.

Asymptotic freedom: at very short distances (high energies) the strong coupling becomes small.

Do this today

Write an analogy for confinement (e.g., quarks connected by a “rubber band” that stretches and snaps, creating new quark–antiquark pairs).

Day 46 – Jets & Partons

High-energy quarks and gluons produced in collisions hadronize into collimated sprays of hadrons called jets.

Parton model: hadrons consist of partons (quarks and gluons) carrying fractions of the hadron’s momentum.

Do this today

Sketch e\(^{+}\)e\(^{-}\) → q\(\bar{q}\) → two back-to-back jets and label the jets.

Day 47 – Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS)

In DIS, high-energy leptons scatter off nucleons, probing their internal structure.

Evidence for point-like constituents (quarks) inside nucleons came from DIS experiments.

Do this today

Write three bullet points summarizing why DIS was crucial for establishing the quark picture of the proton and neutron.

Day 48 – Resonances & Hadron Spectroscopy

Resonant states appear as peaks in invariant mass distributions.

Examples: Δ resonance, J/ψ, Υ, etc. Each resonance is characterized by mass, width, spin, and parity.

Do this today

Explain what it means when an experiment sees a sharp peak at a specific invariant mass value in a distribution.

Day 49 – QCD Week Review

You should now be comfortable with:

  • Hadrons vs elementary particles.
  • Mesons and baryons in the quark model.
  • Confinement, asymptotic freedom, jets, and resonances.

Do this today

Create a one-page QCD cheat sheet summarizing key definitions (hadron, meson, baryon, jet) and core concepts (confinement, asymptotic freedom).

Module 8

Detectors & Experiments (Days 50–56)

Goal: Understand how accelerators and detectors work together to produce and measure particle collisions.

Day 50 – Accelerators & Colliders

Types of experiments:

  • Fixed-target: beam hits stationary target.
  • Colliders: two beams collide head-on.

Colliders maximize CM energy for given beam energy. Luminosity measures collision rate per area per time.

Do this today

Draw a ring collider and label: beam pipes, collision points (detectors), bending magnets, RF cavities.

Day 51 – Tracking Detectors

Tracking detectors measure charged particle trajectories in a magnetic field.

Curvature of track → momentum (higher momentum = straighter track).

Do this today

Sketch a charged track in a magnetic field inside a detector and explain qualitatively how curvature relates to momentum.

Day 52 – Calorimeters & Muon Systems

Calorimeters:

  • EM calorimeter measures electrons/photons via EM showers.
  • Hadronic calorimeter measures hadrons via hadronic showers.

Muon systems detect muons, which penetrate through calorimeters.

Do this today

Draw concentric detector layers: tracker → EM calorimeter → hadronic calorimeter → muon system. Label what each measures.

Day 53 – Trigger & Data Acquisition (DAQ)

Modern colliders produce enormous event rates; we cannot record everything.

Trigger systems (hardware + software) select “interesting” events:

  • Level-1 hardware triggers: very fast and coarse.
  • High-level triggers: more detailed software-based selection.

Do this today

Write a short explanation of why we need triggers and what would happen if we tried to store every collision.

Day 54 – Event Reconstruction & Invariant Mass Peaks

Event reconstruction: infer particle types, momenta, and energies from detector signals.

Invariant mass of combinations of final-state particles is used to search for resonances (peaks).

Do this today

Imagine you reconstruct many e\(^{+}\)e\(^{-}\) pairs and plot their invariant mass. Describe what you’d see if a new particle decays into e\(^{+}\)e\(^{-}\) and how it appears in the distribution.

Day 55 – Landmark Discoveries

Key milestones:

  • W and Z bosons (1983).
  • Top quark (1995).
  • Higgs boson (2012).

Each discovery involved a synergy of theoretical prediction, detector design, and careful data analysis.

Do this today

Create a timeline with at least five big milestones in particle physics, listing the particle and the year of discovery.

Day 56 – From Idea to Publication

Typical analysis workflow:

  • Theoretical motivation and definition of a signal.
  • Design of selection criteria and background estimates.
  • Reconstruction of events and statistical analysis.
  • Comparison of data vs prediction; result & publication.

Do this today

Write a bullet-point list of steps you would follow to search for a hypothetical new particle X in collider data.

Module 9

Beyond the Standard Model & Review (Days 57–60)

Goal: See why the Standard Model is incomplete and consolidate everything you've learned.

Day 57 – Neutrino Oscillations & New Physics

Neutrino oscillations: neutrinos change flavor as they propagate, implying they have mass and mix.

Minimal Standard Model predicted massless neutrinos, so oscillations require new elements (e.g., mass terms, mixing matrices).

Do this today

Write 4–5 bullet points explaining why neutrino oscillations imply physics beyond the original Standard Model.

Day 58 – Dark Matter & Other BSM Ideas

Evidence for dark matter:

  • Galaxy rotation curves.
  • Gravitational lensing.
  • Cosmic microwave background & large-scale structure.

Candidate theories: WIMPs, axions, sterile neutrinos, supersymmetric particles, etc.

Do this today

Summarize in one paragraph: “Why we think there is dark matter, and why the Standard Model does not explain it.”

Day 59 – Global Review & Concept Map

Connect all the major pillars:

  • Relativity + quantum mechanics → need for quantum field theory.
  • Standard Model as a gauge theory of \(SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1)\).
  • QCD and electroweak sectors tested in high-energy experiments.

Do this today

Create a concept map with “Particle Physics” at the center and branches to: Foundations, QM, Relativity, Standard Model, QCD, Experiments, BSM. Under each, list 3–5 keywords.

Day 60 – Final Summary & Self-Assessment

End-of-Course Summary Notes (Quick Review)

Use this as a revision sheet. Expand it with your own notes and examples.

Foundations

  • Relativistic relation: $$E^2 = (pc)^2 + (mc^2)^2$$
  • 4-vectors unify space & time, energy & momentum.
  • Quantum states are vectors; observables are operators.
  • Uncertainty principle: $$\Delta x\,\Delta p \gtrsim \frac{\hbar}{2}$$
  • Wave–particle duality: particles have wavelengths, waves carry quanta.

Standard Model Particles

  • Leptons: three families \((\nu_e,e), (\nu_\mu,\mu), (\nu_\tau,\tau)\).
  • Quarks: \(u,d,c,s,t,b\) with charges \(+2/3\) or \(-1/3\) in units of \(e\).
  • Gauge bosons: γ (EM), W & Z (weak), gluons (strong).
  • Higgs boson: excitation of Higgs field; gives mass via coupling.

Symmetries & Conservation

  • Noether: continuous symmetries ↔ conserved quantities.
  • Conserved: electric charge, baryon & lepton numbers (approx.).
  • Discrete: C, P, T; combined CPT is exact in local QFT.
  • CP violation in weak interactions crucial for matter asymmetry.

Interactions & Observables

  • Feynman diagrams are visual expansions of amplitudes.
  • Decay width \(\Gamma\) and lifetime \(\tau\): \(\Gamma \approx \hbar/\tau\).
  • Cross section \(\sigma\) measures interaction probability (barns).
  • Rates depend on σ, luminosity, and detector efficiency.

QCD & Hadrons

  • Hadrons: mesons (q\(\bar{q}\)), baryons (qqq).
  • Color confinement: no free quarks observed.
  • Asymptotic freedom: strong coupling weakens at high energies.
  • Jets: hadronized partons; important in collider analyses.

Detectors & Experiments

  • Layered detectors: tracker, calorimeters, muon systems.
  • Triggers select interesting events from huge rates.
  • Invariant mass peaks reveal resonances (e.g., W, Z, Higgs).
  • Statistical significance (e.g., 5σ) used to claim discoveries.

Beyond the Standard Model

  • Neutrino oscillations → neutrinos have mass → SM incomplete.
  • Dark matter evidence from astrophysics and cosmology.
  • Candidate frameworks: SUSY, extra dimensions, new gauge sectors, etc.
  • Ongoing experiments search for new particles and interactions.

After this course, natural next steps include: a more mathematical treatment via Quantum Field Theory (QFT), group theory for particle physics, and specialized topics like advanced QCD or neutrino physics.
