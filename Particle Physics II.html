Advanced Particle Physics

Particle Physics II – 60-Day Course

60 Days • Self-paced Focus: QFT basics, gauge theories, Standard Model structure, neutrinos & collider phenomenology.

Course Overview

Big Picture

Goal: Take you from a strong conceptual understanding of particle physics (Particle Physics I) to a level where you can comfortably navigate the language of relativistic quantum fields, gauge theories, and basic collider phenomenology.

You will:

  • Understand how relativistic wave equations (Klein–Gordon, Dirac) motivate quantum field theory (QFT).
  • See how fields, Lagrangians, and symmetries generate Feynman rules and scattering amplitudes.
  • Learn how the Standard Model emerges as a gauge theory of SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1).
  • Explore neutrino mixing and oscillations with real formulas.
  • Connect theory to experiment via collider kinematics, jets, and statistics.
  • Get a first look at Beyond-Standard-Model (BSM) ideas and effective field theory (EFT).

Week-by-Week Breakdown

Roadmap

Use this as your high-level map. Each week corresponds to ~7 daily lessons with concrete tasks.

  • Week 1 (Days 1–7): Relativistic Quantum Mechanics Refresher – Klein–Gordon, Dirac, spinors, interpretation issues.
  • Week 2 (Days 8–14): Quantum Fields & Lagrangians – Action principle, free scalar field, quantization, interactions.
  • Week 3 (Days 15–21): From Fields to Feynman Rules – Propagators, vertices, tree-level 2→2 scattering.
  • Week 4 (Days 22–28): Gauge Theories & QED – U(1) gauge invariance, QED Lagrangian, Ward identities, renormalization idea.
  • Week 5 (Days 29–35): Electroweak Theory & Higgs Mechanism – SU(2)×U(1), SSB, W/Z/γ mixing, Yukawa couplings, CKM/PMNS.
  • Week 6 (Days 36–42): QCD & Running Couplings – Yang–Mills, non-Abelian features, running coupling, confinement, partons, jets.
  • Week 7 (Days 43–49): Neutrino Physics & Flavor – Flavor vs mass states, oscillations, PMNS, CP violation, absolute mass.
  • Week 8 (Days 50–56): Collider Phenomenology & Data Analysis – PDFs, hadron collider kinematics, jets, statistics, discovery strategies.
  • Days 57–60: BSM & Wrap-Up – EFT, SUSY/BSM sketches, global review, roadmap.

Module 1 – Relativistic Quantum Mechanics Refresher

Days 1–7

From non-relativistic Schrödinger to Klein–Gordon and Dirac equations. Understand why QFT is needed.

Day 1 – Why Relativistic QM & QFT?

Key Ideas
  • The non-relativistic Schrödinger equation is not Lorentz invariant and breaks down at high energies.
  • A consistent high-energy theory must respect special relativity, motivating relativistic wave equations.
  • Relativistic quantum mechanics alone faces issues (e.g., negative energies, multi-particle processes), pointing to quantum field theory.
Do This Today

Write a short paragraph explaining why we cannot just use the Schrödinger equation for high-energy particles. Include at least one argument based on special relativity (e.g., finite propagation speed, Lorentz invariance).

Day 2 – Klein–Gordon Equation

Key Ideas
  • Start from the relativistic dispersion relation: \[ E^2 = p^2 c^2 + m^2 c^4. \]
  • Promote energy and momentum to operators: \(E \to i\hbar \partial_t\), \(\vec{p} \to -i\hbar \nabla\).
  • Obtain the Klein–Gordon (KG) equation (in natural units): \[ (\Box + m^2)\,\phi = 0, \] whose plane-wave solutions satisfy the relativistic relation.
Do This Today

Write down the KG equation and identify which term corresponds to the mass. Explain briefly what it means for a plane-wave solution to satisfy the relativistic energy–momentum relation.

Day 3 – Problems with Klein–Gordon as a Single-Particle Equation

Key Ideas
  • The KG equation’s natural current and “probability” density are not positive definite.
  • Negative energy solutions appear and cannot be simply ignored.
  • These issues suggest that KG is better interpreted as a field equation, allowing for particle creation/annihilation.
Do This Today

Explain why a “probability density” that can be negative is unacceptable. Conclude why the KG equation is better used as a field equation rather than a single-particle wave equation.

Day 4 – Dirac Equation (Conceptual Form)

Key Ideas
  • Dirac sought a relativistic equation linear in both time and spatial derivatives: \[ (i\gamma^\mu \partial_\mu - m)\,\psi = 0. \]
  • Spinor fields \( \psi \) naturally describe spin-½ particles.
  • The Dirac equation predicts antiparticles and resolves some probabilistic issues of KG for fermions.
Do This Today

Write the symbolic Dirac equation and briefly describe the role of \( \psi \), \( \gamma^\mu \), and \( m \). In one paragraph, explain what is new here compared to the KG equation (e.g., spin, antiparticles).

Day 5 – Spinors & Probability Current

Key Ideas
  • Spinors are multi-component objects encoding spin and particle/antiparticle degrees of freedom.
  • The conserved current \( j^\mu = \bar{\psi}\gamma^\mu \psi \) has a positive time component \( j^0 \) that can be interpreted as a probability density.
  • The Dirac equation thus provides a consistent probabilistic interpretation for spin-½ particles.
Do This Today

Explain why having a conserved current with positive \( j^0 \) solves the negative-probability problem of the KG equation.

Day 6 – Plane-Wave Solutions of the Dirac Equation

Key Ideas
  • Dirac solutions: \( \psi \sim u_s(p)\,e^{-ip\cdot x} \) for particles and \( \psi \sim v_s(p)\,e^{+ip\cdot x} \) for antiparticles.
  • Index \( s \) labels spin projections (e.g., ↑, ↓).
  • Normalization and spin sums are important for practical calculations, but conceptually these represent particle/antiparticle states with given momentum and spin.
Do This Today

Draw a simple diagram: a momentum vector \( \vec{p} \) and two possible spin states. Write bullet points summarizing “particle” vs “antiparticle” solutions.

Day 7 – Module 1 Review

Key Ideas
  • Klein–Gordon and Dirac equations are relativistic wave equations derived from the energy–momentum relation.
  • KG as a single-particle equation has serious probabilistic problems; Dirac fixes this for spin-½ fermions.
  • The problems with relativistic single-particle QM motivate treating fields as fundamental → QFT.
Do This Today

Create a one-page summary comparing KG and Dirac: equation form, spin, probability interpretation, and role in QFT.

Module 2 – Quantum Fields & Lagrangians

Days 8–14

Fields, actions, and the Lagrangian formulation. Free scalar field theory, quantization, and interaction terms.

Day 8 – Classical Field Lagrangians

Key Ideas
  • Action: \[ S = \int d^4x\,\mathcal{L}. \]
  • Euler–Lagrange equation for fields: \[ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \phi} - \partial_\mu \left( \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial (\partial_\mu \phi)} \right) = 0. \]
  • Free scalar field Lagrangian: \[ \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2} \partial_\mu \phi\,\partial^\mu \phi - \frac{1}{2} m^2 \phi^2. \]
Do This Today

For the free scalar Lagrangian, identify the kinetic term and the mass term, and explain in one paragraph why the action principle is natural in relativistic theories.

Day 9 – Deriving KG from the Lagrangian

Key Ideas
  • Applying the Euler–Lagrange equation to the scalar Lagrangian yields the KG equation.
  • This shows how equations of motion emerge from a symmetry-friendly Lagrangian.
  • Symmetries of the action lead to conservation laws (via Noether’s theorem later).
Do This Today

Perform a partial derivation: vary \( \phi \) in the action, integrate by parts, and show the structure that leads to \( (\Box + m^2)\phi = 0 \). Focus on the logical steps more than algebraic detail.

Day 10 – Quantizing the Scalar Field

Key Ideas
  • Promote $\phi$ and its conjugate momentum $\pi$ to operators: \( \hat{\phi}(x), \hat{\pi}(x) \).
  • Canonical commutation relations: \[ [\hat{\phi}(t,\mathbf{x}), \hat{\pi}(t,\mathbf{y})] = i \delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}). \]
  • Mode expansion introduces creation/annihilation operators \( a_{\mathbf{p}}^\dagger, a_{\mathbf{p}} \).
Do This Today

Write the conceptual mode expansion: \[ \hat{\phi}(x) \sim \int d^3p \left[ a_{\mathbf{p}}\,e^{-ip\cdot x} + a_{\mathbf{p}}^\dagger\,e^{ip\cdot x} \right] \] and indicate which term creates particles and which annihilates them.

Day 11 – Particle Interpretation in QFT

Key Ideas
  • One-particle states: \( |\mathbf{p}\rangle = a_{\mathbf{p}}^\dagger |0\rangle \).
  • Multi-particle states built by applying multiple creation operators to the vacuum.
  • Vacuum \(|0\rangle\) is non-trivial but has no particles; particles are excitations of the field.
Do This Today

In your own words, explain how “particles are excitations of the field” using the creation/annihilation operator picture. Use an analogy such as an ocean and waves.

Day 12 – Interactions in the Lagrangian

Key Ideas
  • Interactions appear as additional terms in the Lagrangian, e.g. \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{int}} = -\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4.\)
  • Coupling constants (like λ) control interaction strength.
  • Interaction terms determine vertex structure in Feynman diagrams.
Do This Today

For \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{int}} = -\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4\), count how many field lines meet at a vertex. Explain why a larger λ corresponds to a stronger interaction.

Day 13 – Path Integral Picture (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • The path integral formulation sums over all field configurations (histories) weighted by \( e^{iS} \).
  • The generating functional \( Z[J] \) encodes correlation functions and thus observables.
  • This viewpoint is often convenient for dealing with symmetries and gauge fixing.
Do This Today

Write an intuitive explanation (no equations required) of what it means to “sum over all possible field configurations.”

Day 14 – Module 2 Review

Key Ideas
  • Fields and Lagrangians provide a compact and symmetry-friendly way to encode dynamics.
  • Quantization turns fields into operators with creation and annihilation operators that generate particle states.
  • Interaction terms in the Lagrangian determine how particles scatter and decay.
Do This Today

Create a mini “dictionary”: for each type of term in a Lagrangian (kinetic, mass, interaction, source), write what it means physically.

Module 3 – From Fields to Feynman Rules

Days 15–21

Correlation functions, propagators, and how Lagrangians generate Feynman rules and amplitudes.

Day 15 – Correlation Functions & Propagators (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • The two-point time-ordered correlation function \(\langle 0| T\{\phi(x)\phi(y)\} |0\rangle\) is the propagator.
  • Propagators encode how disturbances of the field propagate from point to point.
  • In momentum space, scalar propagator is of form \[ \Delta_F(p) \sim \frac{1}{p^2 - m^2 + i\epsilon}. \]
Do This Today

Write the symbolic form of the scalar propagator in momentum space and explain why the pole at \( p^2 = m^2 \) is related to the physical mass of the particle.

Day 16 – Reading Feynman Rules from the Lagrangian

Key Ideas
  • The free part of the Lagrangian determines propagators.
  • The interaction part determines vertex factors (how fields meet at a point).
  • Each type of term in \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{int}}\) maps to a particular vertex rule.
Do This Today

For \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{int}} = -\frac{\lambda}{4!}\phi^4\), write the propagator rule and the vertex factor (symbolically, e.g. \(-i\lambda\)). Focus on the structure even if you ignore numerical prefactors.

Day 17 – Tree-Level Diagrams in φ⁴ Theory

Key Ideas
  • Tree diagrams contain no loops and provide leading-order contributions to scattering amplitudes.
  • In φ⁴ theory, the simplest 2→2 process involves a single four-point vertex.
  • External legs represent incoming and outgoing particles, internal lines represent propagators.
Do This Today

Draw the tree-level diagram for \( \phi\phi \to \phi\phi \) with one 4-point vertex. Label the external momenta \( p_1, p_2, p_3, p_4 \).

Day 18 – s, t, u Channels (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • Different topologies of diagrams correspond to different channels (s, t, u) in 2→2 scattering.
  • Mandelstam variables \( s, t, u \) encode invariant combinations of the external momenta.
  • Identical particles imply symmetry relations among these channels.
Do This Today

Sketch the three channels (s, t, and u) for a scalar exchange process. In one sentence, explain what “channel” means in this context.

Day 19 – Simple Amplitude Structure

Key Ideas
  • In φ⁴ theory, the tree-level amplitude for 2→2 scattering is approximately a constant \(-i\lambda\).
  • In theories with exchange, amplitudes often look like \( \mathcal{M} \sim \dfrac{g^2}{s - m^2} \) (for s-channel) or similar.
  • Resonances appear when the denominator approaches zero (\( s \approx m^2 \)).
Do This Today

Write the schematic amplitude \( \mathcal{M} \sim \dfrac{g^2}{s - m^2} \) for s-channel exchange and describe what happens when \( s \approx m^2 \).

Day 20 – Cross Section from Amplitude (Very Light)

Key Ideas
  • The squared amplitude \(|\mathcal{M}|^2\) enters the formula for differential cross sections.
  • Cross sections also include phase-space factors and flux factors.
  • Conceptually: larger \(|\mathcal{M}|^2\) → larger probability of scattering.
Do This Today

Write a symbolic relation: “Cross section ≈ (phase space factor) × \(|\mathcal{M}|^2\) / (flux).” Explain in words what each factor means.

Day 21 – Module 3 Review

Key Ideas
  • Lagrangians produce Feynman rules, which produce diagrams, which yield amplitudes and ultimately cross sections.
  • Propagators and vertices are the basic building blocks of diagrams.
  • Resonances and channel structure are reflected in the analytic form of amplitudes.
Do This Today

Choose a toy theory (φ⁴ or scalar+Yukawa). Write out the free and interaction parts of the Lagrangian, and describe in words the corresponding propagators and vertices.

Module 4 – Gauge Theories & QED

Days 22–28

From global to local symmetries, U(1) gauge invariance, QED Lagrangian, and the idea of renormalization.

Day 22 – Global vs Local Symmetries

Key Ideas
  • Global U(1) transformation: \( \psi \to e^{i\alpha}\psi \) with constant \(\alpha\).
  • Local U(1): \( \psi(x) \to e^{i\alpha(x)}\psi(x) \), with \(\alpha\) depending on spacetime.
  • Requiring local symmetry forces the introduction of a gauge field \( A_\mu \).
Do This Today

Write the transformation laws for global and local U(1) symmetry and explain why local symmetry “demands” a gauge field.

Day 23 – Covariant Derivative & Minimal Coupling

Key Ideas
  • Introduce covariant derivative: \( D_\mu = \partial_\mu + ie A_\mu \).
  • QED Lagrangian: \[ \mathcal{L} = \bar{\psi}(i\gamma^\mu D_\mu - m)\psi - \frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}. \]
  • Interaction term emerges as \( e\bar{\psi}\gamma^\mu\psi A_\mu \): electron–photon coupling.
Do This Today

Identify in the QED Lagrangian: free fermion term, free photon term, and interaction term. Write them as a 3-row table with short descriptions.

Day 24 – QED Feynman Rules (Qualitative)

Key Ideas
  • Fermion propagator, photon propagator, and the vertex factor \(-ie\gamma^\mu\) arise from the Lagrangian.
  • Charged fermions couple to photons with strength \( e \).
  • Processes like \( e^- e^+ \to \mu^- \mu^+ \) are described by a photon exchange diagram.
Do This Today

Sketch the diagram for \( e^- e^+ \to \mu^- \mu^+ \) via photon exchange and write a symbolic form of the amplitude, highlighting its overall structure (spinor currents and propagator).

Day 25 – Ward Identity & Gauge Invariance (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • Gauge symmetry implies charge conservation in QED.
  • Ward identities relate amplitudes with external photon lines and encode gauge invariance at the amplitude level.
  • Gauge redundancy means some components of \( A_\mu \) are unphysical; physical predictions are gauge independent.
Do This Today

Explain in a short paragraph how gauge symmetry in QED is connected to charge conservation.

Day 26 – Renormalization Idea (Very Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • Loop diagrams produce UV divergences in naive perturbation theory.
  • Renormalization redefines parameters (mass, charge) in terms of physical observables, absorbing infinities.
  • Couplings become scale dependent (running coupling constants).
Do This Today

In your own words, describe what renormalization is trying to accomplish and why it is necessary in QFT.

Day 27 – Infrared & Collinear Radiation (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • Low-energy (soft) and collinear photon emissions lead to infrared divergences.
  • Inclusive observables require summing over real and virtual photon emission, yielding finite results.
  • Physical detectors have finite resolution, naturally enforcing inclusivity.
Do This Today

Write a 3–4 sentence explanation of why both real and virtual photon emissions must be included to obtain finite, physical cross sections.

Day 28 – Module 4 Review

Key Ideas
  • Local U(1) symmetry leads uniquely to QED with a photon as the gauge boson.
  • QED Feynman rules describe interactions between charged fermions and photons.
  • Renormalization and gauge invariance ensure finite, physically meaningful predictions.
Do This Today

Write a half-page summary titled “From U(1) Symmetry to QED,” covering the covariant derivative, the gauge field, and the form of the QED Lagrangian.

Module 5 – Electroweak Theory & Higgs Mechanism

Days 29–35

SU(2)×U(1) structure, spontaneous symmetry breaking, W/Z/γ mixing, fermion masses & mixing matrices.

Day 29 – SU(2)×U(1) Structure

Key Ideas
  • Electroweak gauge group: SU(2)\(_L\) × U(1)\(_Y\).
  • Left-handed fermions form SU(2) doublets; right-handed fermions are SU(2) singlets.
  • Gauge fields: W\(^a_\mu\) (a = 1,2,3) for SU(2), B\(_\mu\) for U(1)\(_Y\).
Do This Today

Write one left-handed lepton doublet \((\nu_e, e)_L\) and one right-handed singlet \( e_R \), and note their SU(2) transformation properties.

Day 30 – Electroweak Lagrangian Skeleton

Key Ideas
  • The full electroweak Lagrangian includes gauge, Higgs, fermion, and Yukawa parts.
  • Gauge kinetic terms for W and B fields; Higgs kinetic & potential terms.
  • Fermions couple to gauge fields via covariant derivatives; Yukawa terms couple Higgs to fermions.
Do This Today

Symbolically write: \[ \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{gauge}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{Higgs}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{fermion}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{Yukawa}} \] and give a one-line description of what each part does.

Day 31 – Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking (SSB)

Key Ideas
  • Higgs potential: \[ V(\Phi) = \mu^2 |\Phi|^2 + \lambda |\Phi|^4, \] with \(\mu^2 < 0\) for SSB.
  • Non-zero vacuum expectation value (VEV) \( v \) minimizes the potential.
  • Gauge symmetry is spontaneously broken; physical spectrum reorganizes into massive and massless fields.
Do This Today

Sketch the “Mexican hat” potential and mark the circle of minima. Explain what is meant by “choosing a vacuum” direction.

Day 32 – W, Z, and Photon Mixing

Key Ideas
  • Charged W bosons: \( W^\pm = (W^1 \mp iW^2)/\sqrt{2}. \)
  • Neutral fields \( W^3_\mu \) and \( B_\mu \) mix to form the Z boson and the photon: \[ A_\mu = \sin\theta_W\,W^3_\mu + \cos\theta_W\,B_\mu, \] \[ Z_\mu = \cos\theta_W\,W^3_\mu - \sin\theta_W\,B_\mu. \]
  • Weak mixing angle \( \theta_W \) encodes the mixture of weak and hypercharge interactions in EM and Z interactions.
Do This Today

Write the above mixing relations and explain which fields correspond to the physical photon and Z boson.

Day 33 – Higgs Mechanism & Mass Terms

Key Ideas
  • After SSB, gauge bosons acquire mass terms from the Higgs kinetic term with non-zero VEV.
  • Gauge boson masses are proportional to the gauge couplings and the Higgs VEV.
  • Gauge invariance remains a symmetry of the underlying theory, even though the vacuum state is not symmetric.
Do This Today

Explain in a short paragraph how gauge bosons can acquire mass without explicitly breaking gauge invariance in the Lagrangian.

Day 34 – Yukawa Couplings & Fermion Masses

Key Ideas
  • Yukawa interactions couple fermion fields to the Higgs field, e.g. \( y_e \bar{L}\Phi e_R + h.c. \).
  • When the Higgs gets a VEV, these terms generate fermion mass terms \( m_e \bar{e}e \) with \( m_e \propto y_e v \).
  • Differences in Yukawa couplings explain the diversity of fermion masses (though not their exact values).
Do This Today

Write the Yukawa term for the electron and show (symbolically) how it becomes a mass term after the Higgs field acquires a VEV.

Day 35 – CKM & PMNS Matrices (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • In the quark sector, mismatch between mass eigenstates and weak interaction eigenstates leads to the CKM matrix.
  • In the lepton sector, a similar mechanism yields the PMNS matrix for neutrinos.
  • These matrices encode flavor mixing and CP violation in charged-current interactions.
Do This Today

Draw a 3×3 box to represent the CKM matrix and label the rows and columns with quark flavors. Explain in words what the CKM matrix does physically.

Module 6 – QCD & Yang–Mills

Days 36–42

Non-Abelian gauge theories, SU(3) color, running coupling, confinement, parton picture and jets.

Day 36 – Non-Abelian Gauge Fields

Key Ideas
  • QCD is based on SU(3) color with eight generators and eight gluon fields.
  • Non-Abelian gauge fields self-interact due to the structure constants \( f^{abc} \).
  • Field strength tensor includes a term quadratic in \( A^a_\mu \): \[ F^a_{\mu\nu} \sim \partial_\mu A^a_\nu - \partial_\nu A^a_\mu + g f^{abc} A^b_\mu A^c_\nu. \]
Do This Today

Make a table comparing QED (U(1)) and QCD (SU(3)) in terms of gauge group, number of gauge bosons, and presence/absence of self-interactions among gauge bosons.

Day 37 – QCD Lagrangian Skeleton

Key Ideas
  • QCD Lagrangian includes: \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{QCD}} = -\frac{1}{4}F^a_{\mu\nu}F^{a\mu\nu} + \bar{q}\left(i\gamma^\mu D_\mu - m_q\right)q. \]
  • Covariant derivative \( D_\mu = \partial_\mu + ig_s T^a A^a_\mu \) couples quarks to gluons.
  • Gauge structure dictates both gluon self-interactions and quark–gluon interactions.
Do This Today

Write down the gauge and quark parts of the QCD Lagrangian symbolically and explain in one sentence what each part describes.

Day 38 – Running Coupling & β-Function (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • The strong coupling \( \alpha_s(Q) \) depends on the energy scale \( Q \).
  • QCD’s β-function is negative, so coupling decreases at high energies (asymptotic freedom).
  • At low energies, coupling grows large and perturbation theory breaks down.
Do This Today

Sketch a graph of \( \alpha_s(Q) \) vs \( Q \) (on a log scale), showing asymptotic freedom at high Q and strong coupling at low Q.

Day 39 – Confinement & Flux Tubes

Key Ideas
  • Static quark–antiquark potential grows roughly linearly with distance at large separations.
  • Color flux lines are squeezed into a tube/string; pulling apart the quarks leads to pair creation.
  • This explains why isolated quarks are not observed: they are confined inside hadrons.
Do This Today

Draw two quarks connected by a string-like flux tube and describe what happens when you try to pull them apart very far.

Day 40 – Partons & PDFs (More Detail)

Key Ideas
  • Hadrons are composed of partons (quarks and gluons) carrying momentum fractions \( x \) of the hadron’s momentum.
  • Parton distribution functions (PDFs) \( f_i(x, Q^2) \) describe the probability density to find parton \( i \) with momentum fraction \( x \) at scale \( Q \).
  • Hadron-level cross sections factorize into PDFs and partonic cross sections: \[ \sigma = \sum_{i,j} \int dx_1 dx_2\, f_i(x_1,Q^2)\,f_j(x_2,Q^2)\,\hat{\sigma}_{ij\to X}(x_1,x_2,Q^2). \]
Do This Today

Write the above factorization formula and explain what the PDFs \( f_i \) and partonic cross sections \( \hat{\sigma}_{ij\to X} \) represent physically.

Day 41 – Jets & Hadronization (More Detail)

Key Ideas
  • High-energy partons radiate gluons and then hadronize into collimated sprays of hadrons called jets.
  • Jet algorithms group energy deposits in the detector into jets, approximating the underlying partons.
  • Jets carry information about the hard scattering process and QCD dynamics.
Do This Today

Draw a simple event with two back-to-back jets in the transverse plane. Label the approximate transverse momenta and annotate the steps: hard scattering → parton shower → hadronization → jets.

Day 42 – Module 6 Review

Key Ideas
  • QCD is a non-Abelian SU(3) gauge theory with self-interacting gluons and color-charged quarks.
  • Running coupling leads to asymptotic freedom at high energies and confinement at low energies.
  • Partons and jets connect partonic QCD to hadron-level observables.
Do This Today

Build a QCD concept map connecting: “SU(3) gauge”, “gluons”, “running coupling”, “confinement”, “partons”, and “jets”, with arrows and short explanations.

Module 7 – Neutrino Physics & Flavor

Days 43–49

Flavor vs mass eigenstates, oscillation probabilities, PMNS matrix, CP violation, and absolute mass scale.

Day 43 – Flavor vs Mass Eigenstates

Key Ideas
  • Neutrinos produced and detected in flavor eigenstates \(|\nu_\alpha\rangle\) (α = e, μ, τ).
  • Propagation is in mass eigenstates \(|\nu_i\rangle\) (i = 1, 2, 3).
  • Relation via PMNS matrix: \[ |\nu_\alpha\rangle = \sum_i U_{\alpha i} |\nu_i\rangle. \]
Do This Today

Write the above relation and explain what the indices α and i represent in words.

Day 44 – Neutrino Oscillation Formula (2-Flavor Case)

Key Ideas
  • In a 2-flavor approximation (ν\(_e\), ν\(_\mu\)), the oscillation probability is: \[ P(\nu_e \to \nu_\mu) = \sin^2(2\theta)\,\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2\,L}{4E}\right), \] where θ is the mixing angle, Δm² is mass-squared difference, L is distance, E is energy.
  • Oscillation pattern depends on L/E and the parameters θ, Δm².
  • Oscillations imply that at least one neutrino mass is non-zero.
Do This Today

Copy the oscillation formula and label each parameter. Explain qualitatively how changing L or E modifies the oscillation pattern.

Day 45 – Experimental Evidence for Oscillations

Key Ideas
  • Solar neutrinos: observed deficit compared to predictions → solved by oscillations.
  • Atmospheric neutrinos: zenith-angle dependence indicates ν\(_\mu\) disappearance via oscillations.
  • Reactor and accelerator experiments confirm oscillations with controlled sources and baselines.
Do This Today

Write four bullet points summarizing: the solar neutrino problem, atmospheric neutrino anomaly, and how oscillations resolve these observations.

Day 46 – 3-Flavor Mixing & PMNS Structure

Key Ideas
  • PMNS matrix is a 3×3 unitary mixing matrix with three mixing angles and (at least) one CP-violating phase.
  • Mass ordering (normal vs inverted) refers to the ordering of \( m_1, m_2, m_3 \).
  • 3-flavor oscillations produce richer patterns than the 2-flavor approximation.
Do This Today

Draw a 3×3 box labeled as the PMNS matrix and compare its general structure to that of the CKM matrix (which one appears more hierarchical?).

Day 47 – CP Violation in the Neutrino Sector (Conceptual)

Key Ideas
  • CP-violating phase in PMNS can cause differences in oscillation probabilities between neutrinos and antineutrinos.
  • Measuring this CP violation is a major goal of long-baseline experiments.
  • Neutrino CP violation might connect to the matter–antimatter asymmetry of the universe (leptogenesis scenarios).
Do This Today

In a short paragraph, explain why measuring CP violation in neutrino oscillations is important for understanding why the universe is dominated by matter.

Day 48 – Absolute Neutrino Mass & Majorana vs Dirac

Key Ideas
  • Oscillations measure mass-squared differences, not absolute masses.
  • Experiments like beta-decay endpoint and neutrinoless double-beta decay probe absolute mass and Majorana nature.
  • Majorana neutrinos are their own antiparticles, which has implications for lepton number violation and leptogenesis.
Do This Today

Explain in simple language what it would mean if neutrinos are Majorana particles and how this might relate to lepton number violation.

Day 49 – Module 7 Review

Key Ideas
  • Mixing between flavor and mass eigenstates leads to neutrino oscillations.
  • PMNS matrix encodes mixing angles and CP phases; oscillation experiments measure its elements.
  • Neutrino physics points clearly to physics beyond the minimal Standard Model.
Do This Today

Create a one-page “Neutrino Physics Cheat Sheet” summarizing key formulas, mixing concepts, and experimental evidence for oscillations.

Module 8 – Collider Phenomenology & Data Analysis

Days 50–56

From parton-level cross sections and PDFs to jets, signatures, event selection, and statistical interpretation.

Day 50 – Parton-Level vs Hadron-Level Cross Sections

Key Ideas
  • At the fundamental level, collisions occur between partons (quarks and gluons) inside hadrons.
  • Hadron-level cross sections integrate over parton distributions as in the factorization formula.

End-of-Course Summary Notes

Quick Review

Use this section as a compact “cheat sheet” for Particle Physics II. Expand it with your own notes as you study.

Relativistic QM → QFT

  • Relativistic energy–momentum relation: \[ E^2 = p^2c^2 + m^2c^4. \]
  • Klein–Gordon & Dirac equations: relativistic wave equations for spin-0 and spin-½ particles.
  • Problems with single-particle interpretation motivate quantum fields and multi-particle states.

QFT Structure

  • Action \( S = \int d^4x\,\mathcal{L} \); Euler–Lagrange equations → field equations.
  • Free Lagrangian → propagators; interaction terms → vertices.
  • Feynman rules encode diagrammatic expansion of amplitudes.

Gauge Theories

  • Local symmetries require gauge fields and covariant derivatives.
  • QED: U(1) gauge → photon and electron–photon coupling.
  • QCD: SU(3) gauge → gluons, color charge, self-interactions.

Electroweak & Higgs

  • SU(2)\(_L\)×U(1)\(_Y\) gauge structure with left-handed doublets and right-handed singlets.
  • Higgs mechanism: SSB gives mass to W, Z, and fermions via Yukawa couplings, leaving the photon massless.
  • CKM & PMNS matrices encode flavor mixing and CP violation.

QCD & Hadrons

  • Non-Abelian SU(3) gauge theory with running coupling and asymptotic freedom.
  • Confinement: quarks and gluons bound inside hadrons; flux tubes and string-like behavior.
  • Partons & PDFs: factorization of hadron-level cross sections into PDFs and partonic cross sections.

Neutrinos & Flavor

  • Flavor states are superpositions of mass states via PMNS matrix.
  • Oscillations: \[ P(\nu_e \to \nu_\mu) = \sin^2(2\theta)\,\sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta m^2 L}{4E}\right). \]
  • CP violation in neutrino sector and absolute mass scale remain key open questions.

Collider Phenomenology

  • Cross sections at hadron colliders: PDFs ⊗ partonic cross sections.
  • Jets, leptons, photons, and MET define experimental signatures.
  • Statistical tools (p-values, σ) used to claim discoveries and set limits.

Beyond the Standard Model & EFT

  • EFT: SM as leading term, with higher-dimension operators capturing low-energy effects of heavy new physics.
  • BSM ideas (SUSY, dark matter, extra dimensions, etc.) address open problems like hierarchy and dark matter.
  • Precision measurements and high-energy searches together probe the limits of the Standard Model.
